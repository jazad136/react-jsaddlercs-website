Selected Topics

Finite State Machines

An introduction along with a set of learn-by-example exercises helped introduce Finite State Machines as a potentially new concept for incoming freshman in an effort to help them form a strong link between the fine-grained expectations of potential inputs and outputs to future assignments. With formal language concepts defined in terms of three simple sets... an alphabet, states, and transitions ... students were trained to think of this way of considering the input  as a fundamental part of design of many programs they may seek to design .

To round the design aspect of programs in, in SENG 1000 we introduced types of programs that benefit more from listed requirements or UML diagrams instead, and discussed what makes certain programs better suited for machine diagrams.

Relatable Concepts

A baseball example was given as a familiar concept with a number of states one player could be in, as "transitions" are made for the player after being at-bat.

Ways to Benefit

Students were shown situations where Finite State Machines could benefit them over other design models. UML in some cases may be difficult to gain intuition from, for example, in implementations containing classes with a variety of dependencies.

Behavior diagrams designed in the fashion of finite state machines help alleviate some of this complexity by representing the same information in a different way. Counterexamples to this were also brought in that showed written examples ("requirements") may hold advantage over finite state machines at times.
